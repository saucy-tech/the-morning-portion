## Reasonable Reasons to Turn Around

Walk through the scene again slowly. The four friends arrived at the house in Capernaum with their paralyzed friend on a mat. The road was passable. The weather was fine. They made it to the address. And then: the crowd. Not just a line out the door — "packed in, no room to receive them, not so much as about the door" (Mark 2:2). Every reasonable person's instinct would have said: come back another time, find another way in, wait until the crowd clears.

Any one of these excuses would have been legitimate. The crowd was real. They weren't doing anything wrong by being there. The blockage wasn't manufactured by an enemy — it was just the reality of how many people wanted to be near Jesus that day.

The text doesn't record any hesitation. It just says they went up.

*"And they uncovered the roof where he was: and when they had broken it up, they let down the bed wherein the sick of the palsy lay."* (Mark 2:4)

They uncovered the roof. They broke it open. They lowered him in. The obstacle didn't win.

## Legitimate Obstacles vs. Actual Dead Ends

What's worth sitting with here is the difference between an obstacle and a dead end. The crowd was an obstacle. The closed door was an obstacle. The roof was an obstacle. None of them were evidence that God wasn't in this. None of them meant: turn around.

We're good at turning legitimate obstacles into final answers. Something doesn't work the first way we try it, and we treat that as information about whether it was supposed to happen at all. The four friends treated the blocked door as information about needing a different entry point, not about whether to keep going.

Where in your life have you stopped at the crowd instead of looking for the roof? Not because the obstacle was actually a wall, but because it was inconvenient enough to serve as a plausible reason to stop.

## Faith Moves First

The four friends didn't explain their plan before they started climbing. There's no scene where they sit down and strategize the roof option. They moved toward the problem and figured it out as they went. That's often how faith actually works — not as a fully mapped plan executed with precision, but as a series of decisions made under pressure by people who've decided that stopping isn't the answer.

The mat on the ground at the end of this story used to carry the man. He walked out carrying it. The roof that blocked entry became the way in. The thing that looked like the end of the story was just the setup for what Jesus was always going to do.

Don't stop at the crowd. Find the roof.
