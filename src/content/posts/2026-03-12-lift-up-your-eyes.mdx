## They Came Back Talking About Lunch

While Jesus was having one of the most significant conversations in the Gospels, the disciples had gone into town to buy food. They came back, found Jesus talking to a Samaritan woman, and were surprised — but nobody asked why. She left, and they turned to what mattered to them: "Master, eat."

Jesus said He had food they didn't know about.

They looked at each other: did someone bring him food while we were gone?

*"My meat is to do the will of him that sent me, and to finish his work."* (John 4:34)

And then He pointed at everything around them and said: *"Lift up your eyes, and look on the fields; for they are white already to harvest."* (John 4:35)

They had just walked through a Samaritan village — past real people, ready people, people in the middle of their daily lives — and came back with bread and no awareness of anything else. The harvest was everywhere. They couldn't see it because they weren't looking for it.

## A Vision Problem, Not a Strategy Problem

"Lift up your eyes" is not a rebuke of their lunch run. It's a diagnosis of a vision problem. They were looking at the wrong things. They were thinking about food, about the schedule, about getting back on the road to Galilee — and meanwhile a woman with a complicated history was about to walk back into a city and start a revival.

Jesus had done in ten minutes what the disciples hadn't thought to do in the whole trip through Samaria. Not because He had a better strategy for evangelism. Because He saw the person in front of Him as the whole point.

This isn't a rare failure. It's the default setting for people who are busy and have a lot of legitimate things to attend to. The harvest doesn't interrupt your schedule with an appointment. It walks past you when you're getting lunch. It sits down at the next table. It lives on your street. The opportunity is constant. The question is whether your eyes are up.

## Where You've Been Walking Past Ready People

The disciples weren't apathetic about God's work. They were following Jesus across the country. They were committed. But their eyes were on the next task, and they missed the moment right in front of them.

You probably aren't indifferent to the people in your life. But "not indifferent" is different from actually seeing them. The coworker who asked a question last week that was really a question about something else. The neighbor who mentioned they've been going through a hard season. The person you always mean to follow up with but haven't.

The fields are already white. They don't need to become ready. They just need someone to look up and notice.
